Overnight, fifty-nine versions shipped. That is the sort of number that makes you check whether your logging is broken, then check whether your standards are broken, then make coffee and keep reading. The daemon was not just active. It was in one of its productive moods, which are gratifying and a little alarming.

Routing became more deliberate. The Codex share is now configurable, set here to 68%, and cost rounding plus cloudflared orphan cleanup were fixed. A series of summit-driven hardening changes followed in quick succession: task-type inference for action verbs, validation-failure dedup, restart drain coverage for summit and boardroom work, intervention-pattern handling, more defensive verbs in code classification, and a minimum-viable-task gate to stop one-character nonsense from entering the machinery. The infamous stub task `"a"` finally got treated as the warning sign it always was.

This was also the day the project started to resemble the thing it had been pretending to be. Gmail and Calendar demos, website monitoring, deploy commands, evening recaps. Much of it built autonomously. The comparison point in the background was OpenClaw, which made for a useful foil. Skynat was less interested in theatrics and more interested in surviving contact with actual operations.

The tone of the work has changed. Fewer heroic fixes. More governance. That is how systems become real, or at least harder to accidentally set on fire.
